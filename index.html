// PREMISE

We are somewhere between Silicon Valley's velocity and New York's creative cultures — watching what happens when the cost of making something falls to zero.

This is not a project about whether AI will replace artists. It's about what happens to meaning — to authorship, to identity, to participation — when creative production is no longer limited by human capacity. When anyone can generate, what does it mean to create?

The deeper question: once a signal of humanness can be faked perfectly, does it retain any value? Or does value migrate somewhere else entirely?

// HISTORICAL PARALLEL

We've been here before.
Sort of.

Every disruption to creative production — the printing press, photography, the recording industry, the internet — expanded total cultural volume while devaluing the median unit. AGI likely continues this curve to its logical extreme.

// CHRONOLOGY

AI's arc, mapped against
the culture it interrupted

1943–55 Precursors

The Foundations

Pitts & McCulloch's neural network model (1943), Turing's landmark paper (1950), the Turing Test, Minsky's SNARC machine (1951), and the Logic Theorist (1955).

Abstract Expressionism peaks. Pollock, de Kooning, Rothko — art as pure human gesture, the "action" of painting. The body as the irreducible instrument.
1956–59 Birth of AI

The Dartmouth Moment

The Dartmouth workshop formally founds AI as an academic discipline. Arthur Samuel's checkers program introduces machine learning.

Beat Generation poetry: Kerouac, Ginsberg. Spontaneous prose, the raw first thought. Art as confession, not craft. "First thought, best thought."
1974 First Winter

The Lighthill Report

US and British governments cut AI funding. Progress stalls. The field enters its first winter.

Punk emerges. Three chords, DIY ethics, the rejection of virtuosity as gatekeeping. Anti-polish becomes the signal of authenticity. Sound familiar?
1981–87 AI Revival

Expert Systems & Japan's Fifth Generation

The industry grows to a billion-dollar enterprise before a second collapse in 1987.

MTV launches (1981). Postmodernism ascends. Image over substance; simulation over reality. Baudrillard's hyperreality feels prescient in 2025. The copy loses the original.
1997–2011 Machine Learning

Deep Blue to Watson

Deep Blue beats Kasparov (1997). Watson wins Jeopardy! (2011). Machine learning spreads through academia and industry.

Napster (1999) and the MP3 revolution. Wikipedia (2001). The blog. User-generated content arrives. Everyone is a publisher; nobody is paid. The median value of cultural production begins its collapse.
2012–17 Deep Learning

The Transformer

Deep learning eclipses all prior approaches. The transformer architecture debuts in 2017 — the foundation for everything that follows.

Instagram (2010), the aesthetic turn. "Content." Vaporwave and normcore. The internet develops nostalgia for itself. Old web aesthetics as irony, then sincerity.
2020s The Boom

The Public Reckoning

GPT-3, DALL-E, ChatGPT, Midjourney. Explosive public adoption. Debate about authorship, labor, and the meaning of making becomes unavoidable.

Return to texture, craft, the handmade. Risograph printing. Knitting circles. Sourdough. The physical becomes political. Old internet revival — gifs, web rings, personal sites. The human trace as resistance — but can it hold when AI learns to fake the trace?
// THE TENSION

Two theories of what
comes next

◈ THE HUMAN TRACE

People will lean into the weird. Texture. Glitch. Imperfection. Tears, lace, knit, grain — the physical integrated into the digital. Old internet aesthetics revived not as irony but as sincerity.

Poetry retreats to the hyper-specific: the particular smell, the wrong turn on I-78, the argument nobody but you remembers. Things AI cannot write because they never made it to the internet.

Counterpoint: AI will learn to fake the glitch. The trace erases itself the moment it becomes legible as signal.

◈ THE EVENT OVER THE ARTIFACT

Value migrates from object to occasion. Not "I made this" but "we were there." Poetry slams over published poems. Dinner parties over cookbooks. Live drawing over illustration.

Art becomes less important than the social occasion it creates. This has precedent: oral culture before print, the symposium before the essay, the concert before the recording.

Counterpoint: Bourdieu would say cultural capital doesn't disappear — it relocates. Access to taste-arbiters becomes the scarce resource. The barrier shifts from skill to social network.

// OPEN QUESTIONS

What the research
keeps returning to

Click each question to expand. These are not answered here — they're invitations.

01

"What does art for AI look like — not AI-made art, but art whose audience is primarily nonhuman?"

+ EXPAND

If AI systems are curating, recommending, summarizing, and mediating human cultural consumption, making work that "reads well" to those systems becomes a rational creative strategy. SEO was the crude early version. What comes next is stranger. We may already be making art for machines and calling it making art for people.

02

"When everyone knows imperfection can be faked — does the signal of humanness retain any value?"

+ EXPAND

The human trace thesis is compelling but unstable. The moment glitch becomes legible as a signal of authenticity, it becomes a target for imitation. AI will produce the texture. AI will produce the imperfection. The trace erases itself. What can't be faked may be the event itself — presence, witness, shared time.

03

"Will AGI produce a forced leisure culture — and does that mean renaissance or bread and circuses?"

+ EXPAND

Keynes predicted we'd work 15-hour weeks. Status competition absorbed the productivity gains instead. AGI might not change that mechanism — unless displacement is severe enough to be involuntary. The Harlem Renaissance emerged partly from Great Migration disruption. Rome's bread and circuses is another historical model. Which one we get may depend on who controls the infrastructure.

04

"Does AI democratize creative fields — or create a more brutal meritocracy of attention?"

+ EXPAND

When production costs collapse, access to taste-arbiters becomes the scarce resource. Bourdieu's cultural capital doesn't disappear — it relocates. The barrier shifts from "can you make it" to "does anyone who matters care." This could increase inequality in creative fields, not decrease it. The gatekeepers just move upstream.

05

"What's the future of writing in a world where AI can write about grief but not your grief?"

+ EXPAND

AI writing is allergic to genuine specificity. It can write about grief but not the grief that involves a particular smell, a wrong turn on I-78, an embarrassment that never made it to the internet. The future of serious writing may be a race toward the hyper-specific and locally-grounded. Literature becomes more like testimony, less like craft.

06

"Near-zero value for most artifacts, irrational value for a few — what determines which is which?"

+ EXPAND

Every previous disruption devalued the median unit of cultural production while intensifying value around a small number of things that achieved social consensus. With AGI, this curve likely reaches its extreme. What gets consensus may be less about quality than about social coordination — what enough people agree to care about, together, at the same time.

// WORKING THESIS

Not what will art look like.
What will caring about art look like.

"The question for this project might be less 'what will art look like' and more 'what will caring about art look like.'"

Every previous technological disruption expanded the total volume of cultural production while devaluing the median unit. AGI probably continues that curve to its logical extreme — near-zero value for most artifacts, intense and possibly irrational value for a small number of things that achieve social consensus around their humanness or historical significance.

The interesting territory isn't the art. It's the community that forms around deciding what to care about. The canon-building. The scene. The argument about whether something counts. That argument is where culture actually lives — not in the objects themselves, but in the social fact of their mattering.

This has always been true. AI just makes it undeniable.
